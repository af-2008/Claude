5 Mistakes Beginners Make

🕐Post at 7:00pm UK
5 Mistakes Beginners Make in Their First Boxing Class
InstagramTikTokFacebook📹 Reel 30–45s
📱 How to Film (iPhone · <15 mins)
Ask the coach to demo each "mistake" — keep it light and slightly humorous. 5 clips, 3–4 seconds each:
  1. Looking at own hands instead of the target (glancing down mid-combo)
  2. Crossing feet when moving (over-stepping sideways)
  3. Dropping the guard after every punch (hands falling after the jab)
  4. Holding breath mid-combination (exaggerate — puff out cheeks)
  5. Swinging too hard too early (wild, looping haymaker)
Film from waist up. Keep the energy light — the goal is relatability, not criticism.
✂️ CapCut Edit (iPhone · Free Plan) — Educational List Reel
  1. Template: No template — build manually. List-format educational content needs precise timing control. Templates will auto-cut in the wrong places and ruin the numbered sequence.
  2. Filter: "Bright" filter (under Filters → Tone) at 80% — or no filter at all. Clean, well-lit, readable. The "5 Mistakes" format is about clarity and relatability, not cinematic mood. A dark moody filter on comedy/educational content kills the tone.
  3. Auto Captions: Turn ON (under Text → Auto Captions). Set style to white text, black outline, bottom third. This is the biggest trend in educational boxing/fitness content in 2026 — auto-captions keep viewers watching even on mute. Edit any errors in the caption editor.
  4. Text overlays: Add numbered labels "1." through "5." — use "Typewriter" animation (Animate → In), white bold text, bottom-left corner. Each number appears 0.5s before the corresponding clip starts. This is the signature visual format for list Reels in 2026.
  5. Effects: "Chromatic Aberration" (under Effects → Distort) — apply for 2 frames (keep it very brief) at each cut between mistakes. This acts as a visual separator that says "next item" without needing a transition. It's a trending micro-effect in boxing/martial arts content and should be subtle, not the "full glitch" version.
  6. Speed: Keep everything at 1× — the humour and relatability only land if people can clearly see each mistake being demonstrated. One exception: the haymaker swing at mistake 5 — add 0.3s of Optical Flow slow-mo at the peak of the swing. That's the money moment.
  7. Audio: "Mystery Girl" by Housecall — currently trending for tutorials and behind-the-scenes content. The light, slightly quirky instrumental energy matches this post's tone perfectly (self-deprecating humour, coaching). Set to 75% volume so the coach's voice or gym audio is still audible underneath.
This is your highest-comment post of the week — people will tag friends who make these exact mistakes. The Chromatic Aberration effect is only 2 frames: if it flickers more than once per cut it's too long. Use the frame-by-frame scrubber to check.

📱 How to Film (iPhone · <15 mins)
Two parts — film in a quiet period or before a session:
  1. Coach to camera (Part 1): Standing in front of the ring, explains 1 key difference of Ukrainian-style boxing in plain English. Suggested topic: the tight defensive shell/high guard vs. standard upright stance. Film 30–45 seconds raw, edit to 15–20 seconds.
  2. Pad round demo (Part 2): Film the technique applied live — close-up on the guard position, then pull out for the full combination. 15–20 seconds of footage.
Edit: coach talking first → cut to live demo. Simple cut, no graphics needed.
✂️ CapCut Edit (iPhone · Free Plan) — Technical Breakdown Reel
  1. Template: No template. Coach-to-camera content must feel direct and unproduced — templates impose visual rhythms that fight against the authority of a straight-to-lens coaching delivery. Build manually for full control.
  2. Filter: "Film 2" or "Teal & Orange" LUT (Filters → Film) at 60% intensity. Cooler tones = more authoritative and technical. This is the visual language of credible coaching content. Avoid warm filters (Seoul, Tokyo) here — those say "community vibes", not "technical expertise."
  3. Effects: "Zoom In" effect (Effects → Basic) on the moment the coach shows the guard position — the camera pushes in slightly on the key teaching moment, directing the viewer's eye. Apply "Halation" pass (Effects → Light) at 20% opacity on the pad work section only — this creates anamorphic-style light bleed on the gloves and pads, making the action shots look cinematic and athletic without heavy production.
  4. Text: Opening title: "MOST GYMS TEACH THIS." — white bold, static (no animation). Hold 1 second. Hard cut to: "WE DON'T." — same style, 1 second. Then Auto Captions for the coach's explanation (turn on under Text → Auto Captions, style: white text, black outline). Static text reads as more authoritative for educational content — animation says "entertaining", no animation says "I know what I'm talking about."
  5. Speed: Coach explanation: 1× (do not speed up or slow down — authenticity requires natural delivery). Pad combo section: Optical Flow speed ramp — 0.5× as the guard is set → 1.2× through the punch combination → 0.7× as the final defensive position is held. This "slow-fast-slow" curve on technique demonstrations is a proven trending format in boxing/martial arts content that gets high save rates (people save to rewatch the technique).
  6. Audio: No background music during the coach-to-camera section — use original audio only. This is intentional. Viewers watching technique breakdowns on mute are already engaged; music competes with the authority of the spoken coaching. Once the pad round demo starts, bring in "Mystery Girl" by Housecall at 15% volume as ambient texture — low enough not to distract, present enough to give the action footage energy. Apply J-Cut: bring the audio in 1 second before the visual cut to the pad work.
High save-rate post: technical breakdowns consistently get saved for later rewatching. The Optical Flow speed ramp on the technique demo is what drives saves — people slow-scrub through it. Make sure the guard position is clearly framed before the punch combination fires. Cover frame: the guard setup shot, not the punch.
